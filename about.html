What is Shade Line?

Shade Line is a visual perception challenge that tests how well your eyes detect subtle differences in color lightness. Each round presents a rectangular field divided into two regions of the same hue — but with a slightly different brightness. There is no visible dividing line; only the shade difference hints at where the boundary lies.

Your task is to click where the two shades meet. The game adapts in real time — nail it consistently and the shades narrow, making each round harder. It is a pure test of perceptual precision.

What is color perception?

Color perception is the ability of the human visual system to detect and distinguish differences in light. While most people can see millions of colors, the ability to discriminate between very similar colors — particularly in lightness — varies significantly from person to person.

Factors such as age, fatigue, screen calibration, and genetics all influence how well you can detect subtle shade differences. Artists, designers, and photographers often train this skill deliberately. Shade Line gives you a way to measure and practice it.

What is a Just Noticeable Difference (JND)?

A Just Noticeable Difference — or JND — is the smallest change in a stimulus that a person can reliably detect. In color science, it describes the minimum difference in hue, saturation, or lightness that the average human eye can distinguish under normal viewing conditions.

Shade Line is built around this concept. As difficulty increases, the lightness difference between the two regions shrinks toward — and eventually below — what most people consider a JND. At the hardest levels, even trained eyes struggle. The boundary is there, but the visual signal is nearly gone.

How good is your color vision?

Most color vision tests focus on distinguishing between different hues — the classic Ishihara plates used to detect color blindness. Shade Line is different: it tests your ability to perceive differences in lightness, a distinct and often overlooked dimension of visual acuity.

A score above 70 suggests strong perceptual sensitivity. Consistently scoring above 85 puts you in a rare category — the kind of visual precision that separates professional colorists from the rest. There is only one way to find out where you stand.
